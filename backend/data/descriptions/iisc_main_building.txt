The Indian Institute of Science (IISc) Main Building is an architectural masterpiece in the Indo-Saracenic style, located in the Malleswaram neighborhood of North Bengaluru. Founded in 1909 through the vision of Jamsetji Tata and inaugurated by the Maharaja of Mysore, IISc is India's premier research institution. The main building, completed in 1911, was designed by British colonial engineers who blended Mughal, Hindu, and Gothic Revival elements into a unified institutional facade.

The building features distinctive onion-shaped domes, pointed arches, intricate jali screens, and tall minarets that combine Islamic geometric patterns with Hindu decorative motifs. The red stone and cream plaster exterior creates a warm, inviting appearance, while the symmetrical layout and grand portico convey academic authority and permanence.

The IISc campus spans 400 acres of verdant landscape, home to rare trees, manicured lawns, and numerous research laboratories. The main building anchors the campus and serves as the administrative center and a symbol of India's scientific aspirations. The Indo-Saracenic style was deliberately chosen to bridge Eastern and Western traditions, reflecting the institution's mission of combining global knowledge with Indian scholarship. The building is a protected heritage structure and one of the finest examples of Indo-Saracenic architecture in South India.
