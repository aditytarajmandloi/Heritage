Lalbagh Botanical Garden is a sprawling 240-acre garden located in South Bengaluru, widely regarded as one of the finest botanical gardens in India. It was originally commissioned by Hyder Ali, the ruler of Mysore, in 1760 and later expanded and enriched by his son Tipu Sultan, who imported exotic plants and trees from countries across the world including France, Persia, Afghanistan, and Turkey.

The garden's most iconic structure is the Glass House, a Victorian-era conservatory modeled after the Crystal Palace in London. It was built in 1889 and hosts the famous biannual Lalbagh Flower Show during Republic Day and Independence Day, attracting hundreds of thousands of visitors. The garden also features a 3,000-million-year-old rocky outcrop of Peninsular Gneiss, one of the oldest exposed rock formations on Earth, which serves as a geological landmark.

Lalbagh houses over 1,800 species of plants, including many rare tropical and sub-tropical specimens. The garden features ornamental flower beds, a lotus pond, an aquarium, a bonsai garden, and towering century-old trees. It was designated as a Government Botanical Garden in 1856 during British rule and has been managed scientifically ever since. Lalbagh is located near the Bull Temple in Basavanagudi and close to Tipu Sultan's Summer Palace, forming the green heart of Bengaluru's heritage landscape.
