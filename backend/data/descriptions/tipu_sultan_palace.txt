Tipu Sultan's Summer Palace, also known as Daria Daulat Bagh, is an exquisite Indo-Islamic wooden palace located in the Chamrajpet neighborhood of Old Bengaluru. Completed in 1791 by Tipu Sultan, the ruler of the Kingdom of Mysore, it served as his summer retreat within the walls of the Bangalore Fort. The palace is one of the finest examples of Indo-Islamic architecture in South India.

The two-storey structure is built almost entirely of teak wood and features intricately carved pillars, cusped arches, and ornamental balconies. The interiors are adorned with vibrant frescoes depicting scenes from Tipu Sultan's battles against the British, floral arabesques, and geometric Islamic patterns. The fluted columns and elegant archways reflect the refined aesthetics of the Mysore Sultanate.

The palace stands near the remnants of the Bangalore Fort and offers visitors a glimpse into the life and reign of one of India's most storied rulers. After Tipu Sultan's defeat in 1799, the British used the palace as an administrative office. Today, it operates as a museum showcasing artifacts from the Mysore Sultanate, including Tipu's personal belongings, weaponry, and historical paintings. It is one of the oldest surviving structures in Bengaluru.
