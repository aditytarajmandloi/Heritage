The Gavi Gangadhareshwara Temple is a remarkable cave temple located in Gavipuram, South Bengaluru. Believed to have been built by Kempe Gowda I during the Vijayanagara Empire era around 1537, this temple is dedicated to Lord Shiva in his form as Gangadhara — the bearer of the holy river Ganga. The temple is carved partially into a natural rock formation, giving it a distinctive cave-like atmosphere that sets it apart from conventional temples.

The temple is renowned for a stunning astronomical phenomenon: on the day of Makar Sankranti (mid-January) each year, the setting sun's rays pass precisely between the horns of a Nandi bull statue positioned outside, illuminating the Shiva Linga inside the inner sanctum. This alignment is evidence of the sophisticated astronomical knowledge of ancient Indian temple architects. The phenomenon draws thousands of devotees and scientists alike every year.

Within the temple complex, visitors can find two massive stone discs representing the sun and the moon, monolithic pillars, and carved panels depicting Shiva's various forms. The temple features classic Dravidian architecture with its mandapam (pillared hall) and a low-roofed garbhagriha (sanctum sanctorum) nestled within the rock cave. It is located near the Bull Temple in Basavanagudi and forms part of Bengaluru's ancient temple heritage trail.
