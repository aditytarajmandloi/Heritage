The Bull Temple, also known as Dodda Basavana Gudi (Big Bull Temple), is a Hindu temple located in Basavanagudi, South Bengaluru. Built in 1537 by Kempe Gowda I, the founder of Bengaluru, the temple is dedicated to Nandi, the sacred bull and vehicle of Lord Shiva. It houses one of the largest monolithic Nandi statues in the world, carved from a single granite boulder measuring about 15 feet in height and 20 feet in length.

The temple is constructed in the Dravidian architectural style, featuring a towering gopuram (gateway tower), ornate stone pillars, and intricate carvings depicting scenes from Hindu mythology. The Nandi idol is coated with a mixture of groundnut oil and charcoal, giving it a distinctive dark sheen. According to local legend, the bull was once a living creature that terrorized farmers by eating their groundnut crops, and was turned to stone by a divine intervention.

The temple is the centerpiece of the annual Kadalekayi Parishe (Groundnut Fair), one of Bengaluru's oldest and most beloved cultural festivals, held in the Bull Temple grounds each November. The temple complex sits at the end of Bull Temple Road, near Bugle Rock Park and in close proximity to Lalbagh Botanical Garden, making it a key part of South Bengaluru's heritage corridor.
